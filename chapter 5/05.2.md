# Good Design = Flexible Software

## Classes Are About Behavior

Classes are primarily about **behavior**, not just data.

You usually create a subclass when:

- The **behavior** of the subclass differs from the superclass

---

## Apply OO Principles to Add Flexibility

Flexibility comes from applying core object-oriented principles:

- Encapsulation
- Abstraction
- Proper responsibility distribution

---

## Letting Go of Bad Design Decisions

### Design Is Iterative

One of the hardest parts of software design is **letting go of your own decisions**.

- Revisit your design when problems appear
- Early decisions may cause later issues
- Be willing to **refactor and improve**

> Good design requires humility. Pride leads to bad design.

---

## Code Once, Review Often

- Continuously evaluate your design
- Identify weak points in your own code
- Improve it without hesitation

Better design now saves **significant time later**.

---

## Encapsulate What Varies

Whenever something in your system is likely to change:

- Isolate it
- Move it away from stable code
- Encapsulate it

This makes your application:

- Easier to modify
- More flexible
- More maintainable

---

## Simple Solutions Are Powerful

- Prefer simple designs over complex ones
- Avoid unnecessary abstractions
- Solve real problems, not hypothetical ones

---

## Handling Variability in Data

When properties vary across objects:

- Use collections (like a `Map`) to store them dynamically

Benefits:

- Fewer methods
- Less rigid structure
- Easier extension without code changes

---

## Subclassing

You typically create subclasses when:

- **Behavior changes**, not just data

Avoid subclassing if:

- Only properties differ
- Behavior remains the same

---

## Learning Design Through Mistakes

Most good designs come from refining bad ones.

- It’s normal to take wrong paths
- Iteration leads to clarity
- Experience improves decision-making

> The best way to learn design is to **build and revise real systems**

---

# Cohesion

## What Is Cohesion?

A **cohesive class**:

- Does one thing well
- Focuses on a single responsibility
- Contains closely related functionality

---

## Definition

**Cohesion** measures how closely related the responsibilities within a class are.

- High cohesion → well-defined responsibilities
- Low cohesion → mixed, unrelated behavior

---

## Characteristics of High Cohesion

- Methods are related to the class purpose
- The class name clearly reflects its behavior
- No unrelated functionality exists inside the class

---

## Identifying Low Cohesion

Ask yourself:

- Do all methods relate to the class name?
- Does anything feel “out of place”?

If yes:

- That behavior likely belongs in another class

---

## Cohesion and Change

A highly cohesive class:

- Has **one reason to change**
- Is easier to maintain
- Is easier to reuse

---

## Cohesion vs Coupling

- High cohesion usually leads to **loose coupling**
- Well-defined classes interact less tightly with others

This results in:

- Easier changes
- Better extensibility
- Improved reusability

---

## Questions About Cohesion

**Q: Is cohesion just about making code easier to change?**  
**A:** Not exactly. Cohesion focuses on how well a class is structured internally. However, high cohesion often leads to easier changes.

---

**Q: Does high cohesion mean loose coupling?**  
**A:** In most cases, yes. Well-defined responsibilities reduce dependencies between classes.

---

**Q: Is high cohesion always better?**  
**A:** Yes. It leads to cleaner design, easier maintenance, and better reuse.

---

# Designing for Change

Each time you modify your system:

- Aim to **increase cohesion**
- Reduce unnecessary responsibilities
- Improve structure incrementally

---

# Knowing When to Stop

## “Good Enough” Software

Perfect software is unrealistic.

You should stop designing when:

- The system works correctly
- The customer is satisfied
- The design is reasonably flexible

Spending too much time chasing perfection is inefficient.

---

## When to Move On

Before finishing a project:

### 1. Ensure the Customer Is Happy

- The system meets requirements
- Functionality is correct

### 2. Ensure the Design Is Flexible

- Uses solid OO principles
- Can handle future changes reasonably well

If both are satisfied:

- The system is **good enough**

---

# Analysis and Design Summary

- Well-designed software is easy to change and extend
- Poor design becomes painful over time
- Always improve weak designs
- Strive for high cohesion in every class
- Continuously refine your system

---

# Core OO Principles

- **Encapsulate what varies**
- **Code to an interface, not an implementation**
- **Each class should have only one reason to change**
- **Classes should focus on behavior and responsibility**
